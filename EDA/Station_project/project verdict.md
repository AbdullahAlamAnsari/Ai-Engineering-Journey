**Verdict: STN\_003 did not suffer a hardware failure on Day 15 — the drop was caused by flooding, not broken equipment.**





Between 14:00 and 19:00, STN\_003's hourly entries collapsed to 4, 7, 11, 19, and 25 — a roughly 90–95% drop compared to its own normal range of 80–100 entries in neighboring hours, and far below every other station, which stayed in the hundreds throughout the same window (e.g., STN\_002 logged 96, 140, 224, 455, and 525 entries in those same hours). If this were a simple sensor/hardware fault, we'd expect entries to flatline at exactly 0 or disappear as missing data (NaN), instead they dropped to a small but non-zero trickle, which looks more like reduced foot traffic than a broken turnstile.



The timing lines up with the weather data: Day 15 recorded 59.4mm of precipitation, the highest single-day reading in the entire 30-day dataset by a wide margin (the next-highest days were in the single digits). Combined with 91.7% humidity that day, this points to a serious flooding or storm event, not a random equipment glitch.



The scooter data backs this up too: near-station scooter activity didn't vanish during the disputed window — riders were still starting trips at 15:00, 16:00, and 17:00 (3, 1, and 1 rides respectively), meaning people were still moving through the area on other transport when the subway numbers cratered. That's inconsistent with an isolated hardware failure at STN\_003, and consistent with commuters avoiding a flooded station and switching to scooters instead.



Conclusion: the Transit Authority's "hardware failure" explanation doesn't hold up. The evidence points to weather driven disruption (likely localized flooding at STN\_003) rather than a broken turnstile, the drop is too gradual, too correlated with a record rainfall day, and too inconsistent with parallel mobility data continuing nearby.
